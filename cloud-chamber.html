Cloud Chamber

Context

While casually scrolling through YouTube videos, I stumbled upon fascinating content about particle physics. This ignited my curiosity, prompting me to wonder if there was a way for me to witness these phenomena myself. A bit of research led me to discover an intriguing experimental apparatus called the Wilson cloud chamber. Realizing its feasibility, I decided to delve deeper by watching YouTube tutorials to try and create one on my own.

What is a cloud chamber?

A cloud chamber is a sealed environment with vapor, often water or alcohol, that's packed beyond its usual capacity. When energetic charged particles like alpha or beta particles zoom through this vapor, they knock electrons off gas molecules, creating a trail of ionized gas particles. If the vapor is just right, these ions become centers for small droplets to gather around, forming a misty trail – like a cloud track. You can see this cloud track for a few seconds as the droplets drift down through the vapor. Different particles make different tracks: alpha particles leave thick, straight tracks, while beta particles create wispy tracks with signs of collisions causing deflections.

Process

The idea of creating a cloud chamber from a simple can initially caught my attention. Yes, you read that right – a can! I was just as surprised as you might be. Intrigued by this unconventional approach and it is the easiest one to make, I decided to give it a try myself. However, my attempt didn't yield the same level of success as shown in the Video [Click Me to Check out this video]!

I used compressed gas to cool the bottom. Since the experiment failed I didn't film a video Lol!

Trail 2!

For a more formal approach, I opted to utilize an electronic component known as a TEC (Thermoelectric Cooling) pad. This advanced device has the capability to transfer heat from one side to another effectively. My strategy involved attaching four TEC cooling pads to the base of the cloud chamber. Each TEC pad was affixed with one end to the base and the other to a dedicated heat sink. These heat sinks were securely held in place by a plate containing cooling jelly.

Unfortunately, I couldn't attain the required level of coldness, leading to only a limited amount of alcohol vapor forming at the chamber's base. It seems the lack of visible trails can be attributed to insufficient cooling and vapor content. However, I'm optimistic about addressing this during the upcoming winter. I plan to revisit the project with fresh determination and will keep you updated on my progress in the future.
